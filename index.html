New York City was known as the Big Oyster. 350 square miles of oyster beds along shores of Brooklyn and Queens and all of Manhattan. A total of 399 oyster reefs. Three Oyster Islands: -Little Oyster Islands – Staten Island and Ellis Island -Great Oyster Island – Liberty Island Oysters were so prevalent half of the world’s oysters were found in the New York’s harbors. Pearl Street earned its name from a midden – oyster shell piles – left from centuries of enjoyment by the Lenape. Osteria virginica.

Oyster prices remained the same for 50 years in New York. The salty water of Long Island Sound and the fresh river streams of the Hudson and East River formed the perfect estuary for the bivalve bounty. Warm brackish water in intertidal areas with fresh water rich in organic material idea environment for oysters to grow. The more oysters eat, the more they grow. Oysters in warmer water grow at faster rate than those in colder water. Ideal temp for oysters is between 68-86 degrees Fahrenheit.

Oysters reefs serve as natural buffers against rising tides and hurricanes. Oysters help to protect the land from storm surges by forming breakwaters that protect the shorelines from erosion. A single oyster can filter between 20-50 gallons of water a single day. By restoring the oyster population, the oysters will be able to filter the entire contents of New York’s estuaries in 3 days.

Billion Oyster Project (BOP) is using aquaculture to restore the oyster population in New York. To date, BOP has placed 24 million oysters in the harbors. Their goal is to plant 2 billion oysters by 2035. Oyster shells collected recycled from New York restaurants are cleaned. The shells are the perfect surface for seedlings to adhere.
