A Brief History

New York City was once known as the oyster capital of the world. Three hundred and fifty square miles of oyster beds ran along shores of Brooklyn and Queens and the entire island of Manhattan. There were 399 oyster reefs. Half of the world’s oysters were found in the New York’s harbors.

Overfishing and pollution destroyed New York's oyster population. Their decline has not only deprived us of a culinary treat, it's also left the city environmentally vulnerable. Oysters reefs serve as natural buffers against rising tides and hurricanes. Oysters help to protect the land from storm surges by forming breakwaters that protect the shorelines from erosion.

Efforts are underway to restore the native oyster population. In the following pages, you will learn about the Billion Oyster Project and the New York Harbor School's work to cultivate oysters with the aim to restore the water quality, reestablish shoreline protections, and educate New Yorkers about the importance of oysters in the life and health of the city.
